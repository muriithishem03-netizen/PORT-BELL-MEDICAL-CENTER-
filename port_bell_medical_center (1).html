First Aid Guide
Symptom & Emergency Navigator

Select an emergency scenario below for instant step-by-step first aid guidance. This is not a substitute for professional medical care — always seek help immediately.

Poisoning

Ingested, inhaled, or skin/eye contact

1
Identify the substance if it is safe to do so. Check for packaging or labels nearby and keep them — they are critical for the medical team.
2
Do not induce vomiting unless specifically instructed by a medical professional. It can cause further harm with certain substances.
3
For skin or eye contact: flush the area immediately with large amounts of clean running water for at least 15–20 minutes. Remove contaminated clothing carefully.
4
If unconscious or struggling to breathe, place the person on their side (recovery position) to keep the airway clear. Monitor breathing constantly.
5
Rush to our facility immediately and bring the substance or its container with you so our doctors can act without delay.
⚠️
Call our Emergency Desk immediately Do not wait for symptoms to worsen. Rush to our facility right now — every minute counts with poisoning.

Choking

Airway obstruction — partial or complete

1
Assess severity. If the person can speak, cough, or breathe, encourage them to keep coughing. Do not intervene — their own cough is most effective.
2
If they cannot speak or breathe, stand behind them, place one foot forward for stability, and make a fist just above the navel and below the breastbone.
3
Perform abdominal thrusts (Heimlich manoeuvre): sharp upward inward thrusts up to 5 times. Check if the airway has cleared after each set.
4
For infants under 1 year: hold face-down on your forearm, give up to 5 firm back blows between shoulder blades. Turn face-up and give up to 5 chest thrusts using two fingers on the centre of the chest.
5
If the person loses consciousness, lower them gently, begin CPR if trained, and look for a visible obstruction before giving rescue breaths. Never do blind finger sweeps.
⚠️
Call our Emergency Desk immediately Rush to our facility right now — even if the obstruction clears, the person must be examined by a doctor.

Heart Attack

Sudden chest pain or cardiac emergency

1
Sit the person down comfortably — on the floor with their back against a wall and knees bent is ideal. Keep them as calm as possible to reduce strain on the heart.
2
Loosen tight clothing around the neck, chest, and waist — collars, ties, and belts. This allows easier breathing and reduces physical strain.
3
If conscious, ask whether they have been prescribed heart medication (aspirin or a nitrate spray). Help them take it if they are able to swallow and have it on them.
4
Do not leave them alone. Reassure them, monitor their condition, and note the time symptoms began — this is critical information for our doctors.
5
If they lose consciousness and stop breathing normally, begin CPR immediately if trained: 30 chest compressions, then 2 rescue breaths. Continue until help arrives.
⚠️
Call our Emergency Desk immediately Rush to our facility right now — a heart attack is life-threatening and requires urgent medical treatment.

Seizures

Convulsions — uncontrolled brain electrical activity

1
Stay calm and note the time. If the seizure lasts more than 5 minutes or the person does not regain consciousness after it ends, this is a medical emergency.
2
Ease them to the ground gently and clear the area of hard, sharp, or dangerous objects that could cause injury during the convulsions.
3
Turn them onto their side (recovery position) to keep the airway clear and prevent choking. Cushion their head with something soft.
4
Do NOT put anything in their mouth and do NOT hold them down. Both are dangerous myths — restraining them can cause serious injury to both of you.
5
After the seizure, speak calmly and reassure them. They will be confused and tired. Do not offer water or food until they are fully alert and conscious.
⚠️
Call our Emergency Desk immediately Rush to our facility right now — especially for a first seizure, a seizure over 5 minutes, or if the person is injured.
